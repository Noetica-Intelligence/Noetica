## Mission

Build an open-source scientific intelligence platform that continuously discovers, ranks, connects, explains, and forecasts the most important developments in human knowledge across all disciplines — using evidence-based signals, not popularity signals.

### The Eight Core Questions Every Discovery Must Answer

1. **What happened?** — The discovery itself
2. **Why does it matter?** — Scientific significance
3. **How reliable is it?** — Evidence strength
4. **How does it connect to existing knowledge?** — Knowledge graph position
5. **What future developments might emerge from it?** — Forecasting
6. **Who should care?** — Personalization
7. **What is its historical significance?** — Timeline placement
8. **What is its civilization-level impact?** — Civilization score


## The Three-Dimensional Scoring System

Importance cannot be known with certainty. It can only be **estimated probabilistically**.

> *"Based on available evidence, what is the probability that this discovery becomes historically significant?"*

### Dimension 1: Scientific Significance Score

| Metric | Weight |
|--------|--------|
| Evidence Strength (replication, validation, stats rigor) | 25% |
| Replication (independent confirmation) | 20% |
| Novelty (semantic distance from existing knowledge) | 15% |
| Long-term Citation Growth | 15% |
| Cross-Disciplinary Influence | 10% |
| Funding & Research Momentum | 10% |
| Practical Impact | 5% |
| **Social media: 0%** | — |

### Dimension 2: Technological Significance Score
- Patent activity
- Industry adoption
- Open-source adoption (GitHub stars, forks, dependents)
- Product creation

### Dimension 3: Civilization Significance Score
- Health impact (lives saved/improved)
- Economic impact (industries created)
- Educational impact
- Energy / environmental impact
- Societal behavior change
- Scientific advancement enabling future discoveries

**These three scores are kept separate. Never blended.**

A discovery with:
```
Scientific Score:     97
Public Interest:       5
```
...still ranks #1.

---

## Novelty Score — How to Measure It

### Semantic Distance
If 95% resembles existing work → Novelty = Low
If introducing new concepts → Novelty = High

### Citation Network Disruption
A discovery is disruptive if future work cites *it* instead of older foundations. (Measurable — see "Disruption Index" by Park et al., 2023)

### Cross-Field Novelty
Transformers applied to AI = Low novelty (already in AI)
Transformers applied to biology = High novelty (new to biology)
Noetica rewards cross-field novelty explicitly.

## The Social Media Signal — Properly Demoted

Social media is a **detector**, not a **judge**.

```
Social Media Signal
       ↓
Potential Trend Detected
       ↓
Trigger Investigation
       ↓
Evidence Analysis
       ↓
Rank via Scientific Score
```

If used in scoring at all:

```
Overall Score weight of Community Attention = 5%
Inside Community Attention:
  Expert Citations         40%
  Conference Discussions   30%
  Research Blogs           15%
  Patents Mentioning It    10%
  Social Media              5%

→ Social media contributes: 5% × 5% = 0.25% of final score
```

**Field-dependent social weight:**

| Field | Max Social Weight |
|-------|-------------------|
| Mathematics | 0% |
| Theoretical Physics | 0.5% |
| Statistics | 1% |
| Biology / Medicine | 2% |
| AI | 3–5% |
| Startups | 5–10% |

A theorem in algebraic geometry can sit for 20 years before the world realizes it's foundational. Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems received zero social media attention (largely because Twitter did not exist, which in retrospect may have been a net benefit for civilization).

## Data Sources Layer

### Tier 1 — Research Papers
- arXiv
- PubMed
- CrossRef
- OpenAlex
- Semantic Scholar
- bioRxiv / medRxiv
- SSRN

### Tier 2 — Forward-Looking Signals
- **Patents** (USPTO, WIPO, Google Patents) — *commercialization intent*
- **Grants** (NIH, NSF, ERC, Horizon Europe) — *where governments see the future*
- **Startup Funding** (VC, Seed, Series) — *where capital is betting*
- **Open Source** (GitHub, GitLab) — *where builders are working*
- **Government Programs** (DARPA, national AI missions, space programs)

### Tier 3 — Community & Conference
- Scientific conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, ISMB, APS, etc.) — *pre-publication signals*
- Major datasets (ImageNet, Human Genome, AlphaFold DB) — *datasets create fields*
- Expert blogs, preprint discussions, academic forums

> A paper tells you what happened.
> A grant tells you what *will* happen.
> A patent tells you what someone wants to *monetize*.
> A startup tells you where *capital* is betting.
> Together they show the future.

## Personalization Engine

### User Inputs
- Interests (e.g., Bioinformatics, Quantum Computing, Statistics)
- Expertise level (Beginner / Intermediate / Expert / Researcher)
- Reading time (5 min / 30 min / 2 hrs per day)
- Career goal (Learning / Research / Startup / Investment / Teaching / Policy)

### The 80/20 Forced Exploration Rule
```
80% — User's stated interests
20% — Outside interests (forced exploration)
```

A bioinformatician should occasionally receive:
- Topology
- Information Theory
- Behavioral Economics
- Philosophy of Science

Not because it's comfortable. Because that is where unexpected connections emerge. Most cross-disciplinary breakthroughs come from someone who read something outside their field that they weren't supposed to be reading.

## AI Analysis Layer (Per Discovery)

| Component | Format |
|-----------|--------|
| Executive Summary | 100 words |
| ELI15 Summary | Simple language, no jargon |
| Expert Technical Breakdown | Methods, Data, Limitations |
| Future Implications | Potential applications, 5-year horizon |
| Cross-Disciplinary Connections | Which other fields are affected and how |
| Competing Theories | What challenges this discovery |
| Related Discoveries | What this builds on / what builds on this |

**Example Cross-Disciplinary Output:**
> A quantum computing paper may affect: cryptography, drug discovery, optimization, finance, climate modelling.

Most paper recommendation systems miss this entirely.

## Technical Architecture

```
Python Layer (Orchestrator + API Glue)
  ├── fetch_papers.py        → arXiv, PubMed, OpenAlex, bioRxiv
  ├── fetch_patents.py       → USPTO, WIPO, Google Patents
  ├── fetch_grants.py        → NIH, NSF, ERC
  ├── fetch_funding.py       → Crunchbase, VC feeds
  ├── fetch_opensource.py    → GitHub trending
  ├── fetch_conferences.py   → NeurIPS, ICML, ISMB proceedings
  │
  ▼ (stdin pipe — JSON)

Zig Engine (High-performance compute core)
  ├── biosignal.zig          → Domain-specific keyword detection
  ├── math.zig               → Full composite scoring
  ├── graph.zig              → Jaccard semantic edges, cross-domain bridges
  └── network.zig            → PageRank centrality, influence ranking
  │
  ▼ (stdout — enriched JSON)

Python Post-processor
  ├── database.py            → SQLite → eventually Postgres
  ├── knowledge_graph.py     → Graph persistence and traversal
  ├── ai_analyst.py          → LLM-based summarization (optional)
  ├── build_email.py         → HTML digest construction
  └── send_email.py          → SMTP delivery

Frontend (GitHub Pages → eventually full app)
  ├── index.html             → Knowledge graph visualization
  ├── app.js                 → 3D force-directed graph (Three.js)
  └── style.css              → Glassmorphic dark mode
```
