[Clinical Trial] Take5Care+: A Self-management Educational Intervention

ClinicalTrials.gov  ·  ClinicalTrials.gov
↳ Aggregated from: 1 trial
1. Abstract Overview:
Many patients newly diagnosed with blood cancers feel a loss of control over their health. This study introduces "Take Five to Age Well," a 30-day program designed to empower them to take charge by adopting healthy habits. It aims to see if this self-management approach is practical and can improve their health and well-being.

2. Research Details:
Topic: This research focuses on "Take5Care+," a behavior change intervention for newly diagnosed patients with less aggressive or chronic types of blood cancer. The program helps patients establish healthy habits over 30 days to improve their self-management of health.
Workflow: Patients are recruited from Northampton General Hospital. They first complete a baseline survey. They then engage in a self-managed, 30-day healthy habit "challenge" at home, supported remotely. Follow-up surveys are conducted immediately after the challenge and again three months later. Some patients and clinicians may also be interviewed.
Material: The primary "material" is the "Take Five to Age Well" program (specifically Take5Care+), which guides patients through adopting healthy actions. Data is collected via surveys completed by patients and optional interviews with patients and their clinicians.
Methods: This is primarily a "feasibility study." Its main goal is to assess if it's practical to recruit these specific patients and if they can successfully participate in and complete the Take5Care+ program. Secondary goals include evaluating the program's impact on patients' self-reported health, well-being, healthy behaviors, sense of control, and health knowledge, as well as gathering perceptions from both patients and clinicians.

3. Key Findings & Future Directions:
This crucial feasibility study will show if a remote, self-managed health program can work for blood cancer patients. If successful, it paves the way for larger trials, potentially empowering patients to gain better control over their health and improve their quality of life.
