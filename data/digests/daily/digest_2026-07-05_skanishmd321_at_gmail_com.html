Evaluating AI-Generated Molecules for Drug Discovery: From Generic Metrics to Translational Readiness

Xiaomeng Liu, Huanxiang Liu  ·  SemanticScholar
1. Abstract Overview:

This paper discusses how Artificial Intelligence (AI) creates potential new drug molecules, but warns that current methods for judging their usefulness aren't good enough. It argues we need better ways to evaluate these AI-generated molecules to see if they're truly practical for drug discovery. Simply generating new ideas isn't enough; we need to know if they'll actually work.





2. Research Details:



Topic: The use of AI to generate novel chemical structures for early-stage drug discovery.



Workflow: AI algorithms propose candidate molecular structures, which are then evaluated.



Material and Methods: Currently, many studies rely on "model-level metrics" such as validity (is it a real molecule?), uniqueness (is it different from others?), novelty (is it new compared to existing ones?), and diversity (how varied are the generated molecules?). The paper highlights that these metrics only describe the AI's ability to produce new and distinct structures, but fail to assess if these molecules are chemically stable, biologically active, or practical to test in a laboratory setting. Essentially, the focus is on critiquing the current, insufficient evaluation methods rather than proposing new specific experimental materials or methods.





3. Key Findings & Future Directions:

The main takeaway is that AI-generated drug molecules must be treated as initial ideas requiring thorough, real-world testing and diverse evidence. We need to move beyond simple computer statistics to truly validate their potential, leading to more credible and actionable candidates for new medicines.
